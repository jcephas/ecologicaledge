CONSTRUCTING COMMUNITY

At a moment when social and economic divides are expanding exponentially, the disconnect between the elite institution of architectural practice and the needs of citizens becomes increasingly obvious. This lack of community-focused design is acutely apparent when looking at East Boston, a neighborhood consistently on the fringes of conversation around how its environment is shaped. As members of academic institutions, we often use such neighborhoods as playgrounds for projection of our architectural vision, but seldom take the time to truly understandthe communities we design in service of.

I will explore this issue through the lens of a Community Design Center and a Northeastern affiliated Design - Build program in East Boston. This entails creatingthe framework for production and implementation of the program, coordination of anetwork of community organizers and fabricators, and development of the anchoring site located at 170 Border Street. This site will contain the physical space from which the Community Development Center will engage and empower the community, as well as anchor the network of students and East Boston fabricators.

The project stakeholders include: Northeastern University, the School of Architecture, and Northeastern’s architecture students, as well as the community of East Boston. Specific focus is placed on members of the fabrication and community development industries, and most importantly, anyone who could benefit from improved community services and thoughtful intervention to the built environment. Through the physical network of transportation systems, and conceptual networks of community organizers, fabricators, and students, this project has the potential to create impact at a variety of scales ranging from the physical site, to the entirety of East Boston, and beyond to Northeastern’s campus. Through the implementation of localized, community-centered design in the academic context, we as designers can begin to bridge the gap between what we create, and what our communities need, starting here in East Boston.
