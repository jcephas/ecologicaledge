La Constelación

Designing for the Safety of Undocumented Immigrants

In a country unjustly founded by immigrants, the term "illegal" is unjust for undocumented people in the U.S. The nation's immigration system has made it almost impossible for people who are fleeing difficult situations or looking for better opportunities, to establish homes away from their origin countries. In East Boston and all over the nation, ICE arrests have been occuring more often and breaking the very sense that immigrants came in search for - safety. Despite East Boston declaring itself a "Sanctuary City," a simple policy has proven not to be enough in the protection of undocumented folks.

How can sanctuary cities establish safety for
undocumented immigrants through design?

In order to create a sense of safety, there must be safe places for individuals. Through the use of community ties with leaders and organizations, safe locations, a series of mappings and case studies, a network of safety can be created in East Boston. We have more agency than we think we do. Undocumented immigrant right should not be up to solely national government policy. We can do more - from Policymakers, to planners/designers, to even a community member - we can all do something more in the protection of undocumented folks. The goal of this project is two-fold: (1) to create awareness on the unseen boundaries placed on undocumented folks on the daily, and (2) to propose a framework and guide that could break down these barriers and create a safer environment and community for immigrants.

SITE NARRATIVE

EAST BOSTON BACKGROUND

East Boston has had many changes in population throughout time and it's current population is more than 50% foreign born, with majority being from El Salvador and Colombia. Despite the rise in immigrant population and Boston's Sanctuary City Policy, undocumented immigrants are still being targeted by ICE and deported. This project aims to bring light to the issue, understanding the restrictions that this demographic goes through and looking at the spatial strategies that could be implemented in tackling these issues.

SANCTUARY CITY

WHAT IS A SANCTUARY CITY?

In a city without a Sanctuary policy, there are two main ways that ICE can get to undocumented immigrants, which are through local data basess (such as DMV) and local police reporting.



Cities with Sanctuary policy remove one of the main ways that ICE can find an undocumented individual. This means that local police and databases are prohibited from reporting these individuals.

A GUIDE TO URBAN PLANNING FOR UNDOCUMENTED IMMIGRANTS

Problem: An Unheard Voice

Because undocumented immigrant rights are typically looked as a federal government issue, the population is usually overlooked. This leads to a whole demographic not having a say in public meetings or engagements for new design proposals in their neighborhoods.

Strategy: Sensitive / Safe Locations

Planners need to ensure that undocumented immigrants are protected during community meetings and engagement events. So these public engagements should be done in trusted or Sensitive Locations, like the ones identified below.

A GUIDE TO URBAN PLANNING FOR UNDOCUMENTED IMMIGRANTS

Problem: Restriction in Movement

In Massachusetts, as in the majority of the states in the U.S., people without legal status are restricted from attaining identification or licenses. This limits their movement and the distances that they can travel.

Strategy: Walkable & Accessible Neighborhoods

Undocumented folks only (legal) transportation method is through walking or public transportation. This means making sure the appropriate resources are available to them.

A GUIDE TO URBAN PLANNING FOR UNDOCUMENTED IMMIGRANTS

Problem: Designing for Who?

In America, housing is typically designed for a homogenous type of living, with the family household consisting of usually 4 members. But, many immigrant groups tend to live together in multigenerational households.

Strategy: Designing for Diversity

Designing for multigenerational living and being able to adapt current buildings into better suited for different age groups. Also, planning for streets to be part of the public realm for play, commerce and cultural events.
